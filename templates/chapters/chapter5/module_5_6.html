Hazardous Materials (Hazmat)

Hazardous materials are substances posing risks to health, safety, property, or the environment during transportation. They're heavily regulated and require specialized knowledge.

The Nine DOTDOT (Department of Transportation)The federal parent agency overseeing transportation policy, including FMCSA and other modal administrations. Hazmat Classes

The Department of Transportation classifies hazardous materials into nine classes:

Class 1 - Explosives

  • Division 1.1: Mass explosion hazard (dynamite, TNT)
  • Division 1.2: Projection hazard (aerial flares, torpedoes)
  • Division 1.3: Fire hazard (liquid fuel ammunition)
  • Division 1.4: Minor explosion hazard (small arms ammunition)
  • Division 1.5: Very insensitive explosives
  • Division 1.6: Extremely insensitive detonating substances

Class 2 - Gases

  • Division 2.1: Flammable gases (propane, butane, hydrogen)
  • Division 2.2: Non-flammable, non-toxic gases (nitrogen, carbon dioxide)
  • Division 2.3: Toxic gases (chlorine, ammonia, hydrogen sulfide)

Class 3 - Flammable Liquids

  • Gasoline, diesel fuel, paint, alcohols, acetone

Class 4 - Flammable Solids

  • Division 4.1: Flammable solids (matches, sulfur)
  • Division 4.2: Spontaneously combustible materials (phosphorus, activated carbon)
  • Division 4.3: Dangerous when wet (sodium, calcium carbide)

Class 5 - Oxidizers and Organic Peroxides

  • Division 5.1: Oxidizers (ammonium nitrate, hydrogen peroxide)
  • Division 5.2: Organic peroxides (benzoyl peroxide)

Class 6 - Toxic and Infectious Substances

  • Division 6.1: Toxic substances (pesticides, cyanides)
  • Division 6.2: Infectious substances (medical waste, clinical specimens)

Class 7 - Radioactive Materials

  • Medical isotopes, industrial radiography sources, uranium

Class 8 - Corrosive Substances

  • Acids, bases, battery fluid, mercury

Class 9 - Miscellaneous Dangerous Substances

  • Materials not fitting other classes (lithium batteries, dry ice, asbestos)

Understanding these classes helps you recognize hazmat when customers describe freight and ask appropriate questions.

Hazmat Requirements

Transporting hazardous materials requires compliance with extensive regulations:

Driver Endorsement

Drivers must have a hazmat endorsement on their CDL, which requires background checks, fingerprinting, and additional testing. Not all drivers have this endorsement.

Shipping Papers

Hazmat shipments require detailed documentation including:

  • Proper shipping name
  • Hazard class
  • UN/NA identification number
  • Packing group
  • Emergency response information

Placarding

Vehicles carrying hazmat must display diamond-shaped placards indicating the hazard class. Placard requirements depend on quantity and hazard class.

Practical application

Questions to ask when hazmat might be involved:

  • "Is any of this freight classified as hazardous?" Customers don't always volunteer this information.
  • "Do you have the UN/NA number and proper shipping name?" Required for all hazmat shipments.
  • "What class and division is this?" Determines placarding and handling requirements.
  • "Do you have the shipping papers prepared?" Hazmat requires specific documentation.

Where to focus

For new agents, prioritize:

  • Recognition: Learn to identify common hazmat products (paint, aerosols, batteries, cleaning chemicals, flammable liquids).
  • Ask the question: Always ask if freight is hazardous. Customers sometimes forget to mention it.
  • Know your limits: Complex hazmat shipments require expertise. Ask for help when needed.
