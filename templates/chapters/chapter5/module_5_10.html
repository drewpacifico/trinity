Partial Loads and LTL Considerations

Not all freight fills entire trailers. Understanding partial truckload and less-than-truckload (LTL) options helps you serve customers with smaller shipment needs.

Less-Than-Truckload (LTL)

LTL carriersCarrierA company or individual that transports goods from one place to another, including asset-based carriers, owner-operators, and fleet owners. consolidate freight from multiple shippersShipperA business needing to move goods from one location to another, including manufacturers, distributors, retailers, and other customer types. into single trailers. Each shipper pays for the space their freight occupies rather than paying for an entire truck.

Typical LTL characteristics:

  • Shipments from 150 pounds to 10,000 pounds
  • Multiple handling points (origin terminal, potential intermediate terminals, destination terminal)
  • Longer transit times than truckload (due to multiple stops and terminal handling)
  • Higher cost per pound than truckload but lower total cost for small shipments

LTL works well for:

  • Small businesses without full truckload volumes
  • Occasional shipments of moderate-sized freight
  • Multiple small deliveries to different destinations
  • Freight weighing a few thousand pounds

Partial Truckload

Partial truckload falls between LTL and full truckloadâ€”typically 5-20 pallets or 2,000-12,000 pounds. Too large for LTL to be economical but not enough to fill a truck.

Options for partial loads:

  • Shared truckload: Some carriers offer shared truck programs where they combine 2-3 compatible partial loads heading in the same general direction. Customers share truck space and costs.
  • Volume LTL: LTL carriers offer discounted rates for larger shipments that don't require full trucks but generate good revenue per stop.
  • Partial truck rates: Some truckload carriers accept partial loads at adjusted rates (higher per pound than full truckload but less than you'd pay for wasted truck space).
  • Consolidation services: Some brokerages specialize in consolidating multiple customers' partial loads into full trucks.

LTL Freight Classification

LTL pricing uses a classification system (Classes 50 through 500) based on four factors:

  • Density: Weight per cubic foot. Denser freight generally has lower classifications (lower class numbers mean lower rates).
  • Stowability: How easily the freight stacks and fits with other freight. Irregular shapes are harder to stow.
  • Handling: Special handling requirements increase classification.
  • Liability: High-value or fragile items that create claim risk have higher classifications.
💡 Pro Tip
Freight class significantly affects LTL pricing. Accurate classification prevents billing disputes and reclassification charges. When in doubt, confirm the commodity's NMFC (National Motor Freight Classification) code with the shipper.

LTL AccessorialAccessorial ChargesAdditional fees charged by carriers for services beyond standard dock-to-dock delivery, such as liftgate service, inside delivery, residential delivery, or appointment scheduling. Charges

LTL carriers commonly charge extra for services beyond basic dock-to-dock delivery:

  • Liftgate: $50-150 for hydraulic lift service at pickup or delivery
  • Inside delivery: $75-200 for delivery beyond the truck tailgate
  • Residential delivery: $75-150 for home delivery (higher handling costs)
  • Limited access: $50-100 for difficult-to-reach locations
  • Appointment/notification: $25-75 for scheduled delivery windows
  • Reweigh/reclass: Charges if actual weight or class differs from quoted

Choosing Between LTL and Truckload

Consider these factors when advising customers:

  • Weight: Generally, 8,000-10,000+ pounds becomes more economical as truckload
  • Destinations: Multiple stops favor LTL; single destination favors truckload
  • Speed: Truckload is faster (direct); LTL adds terminal handling time
  • Handling: Fragile freight may do better in dedicated truckload (less handling)
  • Cost sensitivity: Compare total costs including all accessorials

Practical application

Questions to ask for partial loads:

  • "How many pallets and what's the total weight?" Helps determine if LTL or partial truckload is more economical.
  • "How time-sensitive is this?" LTL is slower. Truckload is faster but may cost more.
  • "What's the freight class?" LTL pricing depends heavily on classification.
  • "Any special handling or delivery requirements?" LTL accessorials add up quickly.

Where to focus

For new agents, prioritize:

  • Know the crossover point: Around 8,000-10,000 pounds, truckload often becomes more economical than LTL.
  • Understand accessorials: LiftgateLiftgateHydraulic platform attached to trailers that raises and lowers freight at locations lacking loading docks, with 3,000-5,000 pound capacity limits., residential, inside delivery, and limited access charges can double LTL costs.
  • Offer options: Present both LTL and partial truckload quotes so customers can choose.

Understanding LTL helps you serve smaller customers and provide solutions when truckload doesn't make sense. Not every load fills a truck.
