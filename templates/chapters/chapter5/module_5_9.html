Module 5.9

Oversized and Overweight Loads

Freight exceeding legal size or weight limits requires special permits, routing, and often specialized equipment. Understanding oversize/overweight (OS/OW) requirements helps you recognize when standard freight becomes specialized.

Legal Limits Without Permits

Remember these standard limits—freight within these parameters doesn't require special permits:

  • Width: 8.5 feet (102 inches)
  • Height: 13.5-14 feet (varies by state and sometimes by specific roads)
  • Length: 53 feet for trailers on interstate highways
  • Weight: 80,000 pounds gross vehicle weight

Freight exceeding any of these requires permits and possibly specialized equipment.

⚠ Important
Never accept OS/OW loads with immediate pickup requirements. Permits take time: routine permits need 3-7 days minimum, complex permits 2-4 weeks or more. Route surveys, escort arrangements, and specialized rigging all require advance planning. Customers expecting quick turnaround don't understand regulatory requirements.

Permit Requirements

Each state issues its own oversize/overweight permits. Interstate moves require permits from every state the shipment travels through.

Permit complexity and cost vary based on how much freight exceeds standard dimensions:

  • Slightly oversize (width 8.5' to 10', height 14' to 15'): Routine permits, relatively quick processing (often same-day or next-day), moderate costs ($100-300 per state typically).
  • Moderately oversize (width 10' to 12', height 15' to 16'): May require route surveys, longer processing times (days to weeks), higher costs ($300-800 per state often).
  • Extremely oversize (width over 12', height over 16'): Almost always require route surveys, can take weeks or months for approval, very high costs (thousands per state possible), may require police escorts.

Lead time matters enormously. Don't accept OS/OW loads requiring immediate pickup—permits take time to obtain and process.

Route Surveys

Very large loads may require route surveys identifying obstacles along the planned path:

  • Low bridges and overpasses
  • Narrow roads or tight turns
  • Utility lines that may need temporary removal
  • Weight-restricted bridges
  • Construction zones

Route surveys add time and cost but prevent loads from getting stuck mid-route at impassable obstacles.

Escort Vehicles

Large oversize loads often require escort vehicles (pilot cars) traveling ahead or behind:

  • Warn other traffic of oversized load
  • Help navigate through obstacles
  • Required by permit conditions for loads over certain dimensions
  • Typically cost $200-400 per day per vehicle
  • Some loads require police escorts in addition to or instead of pilot cars

Travel Restrictions

Oversize loads often face travel restrictions:

  • Nighttime restrictions: Some permits prohibit travel after dark
  • Weekend restrictions: No travel on weekends or holidays
  • Weather restrictions: No travel in rain, snow, or high winds
  • Rush hour restrictions: No travel during peak traffic periods
💡 Pro Tip
OS/OW loads command premium rates reflecting permits, escorts, specialized equipment, and extended transit times. Factor all these costs when quoting, and always build in buffer time for permit processing.

Practical application

Questions to ask when freight might be oversized:

  • "What are the exact dimensions when loaded?" Include the trailer deck height in your calculations.
  • "What is the total weight?" Over 80,000 pounds requires permits.
  • "What is the pickup timeline?" Permits take days to weeks. Rush pickups aren't possible.
  • "Have you shipped this before?" Previous experience may reveal permit or route requirements.

Where to focus

For new agents, prioritize:

  • Know the limits: Width: 8.5 feet. Height: 13.5-14 feet. Weight: 80,000 pounds. Anything over requires permits.
  • Lead time awareness: Never promise quick pickup for oversized freight. Permits take time.
  • Ask for help: Complex OS/OW loads require specialized expertise. Know when to involve experienced colleagues.

Oversized freight commands premium rates but requires advance planning. The most common mistake is promising timelines that permit processing won't allow.
