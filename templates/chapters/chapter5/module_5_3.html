Food and Beverage Products

Food and beverage freight represents a large segment of the market with specific requirements you must understand to serve customers effectively and avoid costly mistakes.

Temperature-Sensitive Products

Many food products require precise temperature control throughout transportation. Getting temperatures wrong can spoil products worth tens of thousands of dollars and create serious food safety issues.

Frozen Foods (0°F to -10°F)

Frozen products must remain solidly frozen throughout transit. This includes:

  • Ice cream and frozen desserts: -10°F to -20°F (coldest requirements in food transport)
  • Frozen vegetables and fruits: 0°F
  • Frozen meat, poultry, and seafood: 0°F or below
  • Frozen prepared meals: 0°F

Any temperature rise above freezing compromises product quality and safety. Customers receiving partially thawed frozen products will reject the load, leaving you responsible for resolving the situation.

Fresh Meat and Poultry (28°F to 32°F)

Fresh (not frozen) meat and poultry require temperatures as cold as possible without actually freezing. This narrow range preserves freshness while preventing freezing damage. These products are highly perishable—even small temperature excursions affect shelf life and safety.

Dairy Products (34°F to 38°F)

Dairy products require consistent cool temperatures but not freezing:

  • Fluid milk: 34-38°F
  • Cheese: 35-40°F (some varieties tolerate slightly warmer)
  • Butter and margarine: 35-40°F
  • Yogurt: 36-40°F

Dairy is particularly sensitive to temperature abuse. Temperatures too warm accelerate spoilage; temperatures too cold can affect texture and quality.

Fresh Produce (Highly Variable)

Different fruits and vegetables require vastly different temperatures:

  • Leafy greens: 32-34°F
  • Berries: 32-34°F
  • Apples: 30-32°F
  • Bananas: 56-58°F (cold damages them)
  • Tomatoes: 55-60°F
  • Potatoes: 45-50°F

Practical application

Questions to ask when booking food and beverage freight:

  • "What exact temperature do you require?" Get a specific number, not "cold" or "frozen."
  • "Is this product pre-cooled to temperature?" Reefers maintain temperature; they don't rapidly cool freight.
  • "Do you require a food-grade trailer?" Many shippers have restrictions on previous loads.
  • "What documentation do you need?" Temperature recorders, pulp tests, or specific paperwork.

Where to focus

For new agents, prioritize:

  • Temperature specificity: Every food product has an ideal temperature. Learn the common ones (frozen: 0°F, dairy: 34-38°F, produce: varies widely).
  • Pre-cooling importance: Emphasize this with every reefer load. Most temperature claims stem from improper pre-cooling.
  • Higher rates: Reefer freight costs 20-40% more than dry van. Factor this into quotes.

Food freight has higher stakes because temperature failures can destroy entire loads worth tens of thousands of dollars.
