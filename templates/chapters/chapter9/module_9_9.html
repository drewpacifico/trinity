Module 9.9

Follow-Up with Existing Customers

Follow-up isn't just for prospects. Maintaining contact with existing customers prevents churn and identifies growth opportunities.

Active Customer Follow-Up Schedule

Before Every Shipment

  • Confirm pickup and delivery details (addresses, dates, times)
  • Verify any special requirements or changes from previous shipments
  • Set expectations for communication during transit
  • Ensure customer knows how to reach you if issues arise

During Every Shipment

  • Confirm pickup completed successfully (call or text within 30 minutes of scheduled pickup)
  • Provide in-transit updates (at minimum, mid-transit check on longer hauls)
  • Alert immediately to any issues or potential delays
  • Proactive communication prevents anxious customer calls

After Every Shipment

  • Confirm successful delivery within 1-2 hours of completion
  • Address any concerns or issues that arose during transit
  • Request feedback on carrierCarrierA company or individual that transports goods from one place to another, including asset-based carriers, owner-operators, and fleet owners. performance
  • Send proof of deliveryProof of Delivery (POD)Signed documentation confirming freight was delivered, required for invoicing customers and proving shipment completion. documentation promptly

This per-shipment communication builds trust and demonstrates attentiveness.

Weekly for High-Volume Customers (Tier A)

Schedule regular weekly calls or emails:

"Hi Jennifer, this is our weekly check-in. All five shipments this week delivered on time with no issues. Looking ahead to next week, I see you have seven loads scheduled. Do you anticipate any additional shipments or changes to what's scheduled?"

Weekly touchpoints allow you to:

  • Review recent performance
  • Address any developing concerns before they become major issues
  • Understand upcoming needs
  • Share relevant market intelligence
  • Maintain strong relationship through consistent contact

Monthly for Moderate-Volume Customers (Tier B)

Schedule monthly calls reviewing overall performance:

"Hi Tom, wanted to touch base for our monthly check-in. Last month we handled 12 shipments with 100% on-time delivery. I noticed three of those were new lanes we hadn't handled before—how did those go from your perspective? Anything we could improve?"

Monthly reviews for mid-tier customers keep relationships strong without overwhelming them with excessive contact.

Quarterly for All Customers

Conduct formal business reviews every three months with significant customers:

Review Past Quarter

  • Total shipments handled
  • Performance metrics (on-time percentage, damage rates, issue resolution time)
  • Cost savings achieved or rate trends
  • Challenges encountered and how resolved
  • Customer satisfaction with service quality

Plan Upcoming Quarter

  • Anticipated volume changes
  • New lanes or services needed
  • Market trends affecting their business
  • Seasonal considerations or known busy periods
  • Capacity strategies for peak periods

Discuss Improvement Opportunities

  • Could freight be consolidated for cost savings?
  • Mode optimization possibilities (truckload vs. LTLLess-Than-Truckload (LTL)Freight service consolidating shipments from multiple shippers (typically 150-10,000 pounds), priced by freight class based on density, stowability, handling, and liability., direct vs. intermodal)?
  • Routing improvements for speed or cost?
  • Technology or process enhancements?
  • Additional services that might benefit them?

Strengthen Relationship

  • Acknowledge what's working well
  • Address any dissatisfaction or concerns
  • Discuss their business goals and how you can support them
  • Express appreciation for their business

Annual Reviews for Major Customers

With your largest customers, conduct comprehensive annual reviews:

  • Deep-dive analysis: Full year performance data, trend analysis, cost comparisons
  • Strategic planning: Next year's projections, growth opportunities, service expansion
  • Relationship health: Overall satisfaction assessment, competitive positioning, partnership strength
  • Value demonstration: Quantify the value you've provided (savings achieved, problems solved, efficiencies gained)

Service Recovery Follow-Up

When problems occur, follow-up becomes especially critical:

Immediate (During Issue)

Frequent updates until resolved—over-communicate rather than under-communicate.

24 Hours After Resolution

Call to confirm they're satisfied with how the issue was handled.

One Week After Issue

Send written summary of what happened, what you did to resolve it, and what you're doing to prevent recurrence.

One Month After Issue

Check in to ensure no lingering concerns and that subsequent shipments have gone smoothly.

★ Key Takeaway
Customers who experience problems that are handled well often become more loyal than customers who never experienced problems. The key is thorough follow-up demonstrating you take issues seriously and are committed to continuous improvement.
