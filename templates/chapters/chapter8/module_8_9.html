Module 8.9

Upselling and Cross-Selling

Once you've established trust and delivered value, look for opportunities to expand your relationship by offering additional services.

Upselling Opportunities

Upselling means increasing the value or volume of services customers already purchase from you.

Additional Lanes

Customer ships Chicago-to-Atlanta with you and is satisfied. You notice they also ship to Florida with another provider.

"You've been happy with our Chicago-to-Atlanta service. Would you like us to quote your Florida shipments as well? We have strong carrierCarrierA company or individual that transports goods from one place to another, including asset-based carriers, owner-operators, and fleet owners. relationships on that corridor."

Increased Frequency

Customer ships twice monthly but their business is growing.

"If your shipping frequency increased to weekly, we could likely negotiate better rates due to higher volume. Would that fit your production schedule?"

Premium Services

Customer uses standard service but occasionally has urgent needs.

"For your most time-sensitive shipments, we offer expeditedExpedited FreightShipments requiring faster-than-normal transit, often using team drivers to cover 1,000-1,500 miles in 24 hours. team service guaranteeing delivery in half the transit time. Would having that option for emergencies be valuable?"

Volume Commitments

Customer provides variable volume, sometimes using you, sometimes competitors.

"If you committed to 10 loads monthly for six months, I could offer 8-10% better rates. The volume commitment allows me to negotiate better carrier contracts."

Cross-Selling Opportunities

Cross-selling means offering different services or equipment types than what customers currently purchase.

Additional Equipment Types

Customer ships dry vanDry Van TrailerThe most common trailer type: an enclosed rectangular box (53'×8.5'×9' interior) with 43,000-45,000 lb capacity, used for general freight not requiring temperature control. but also has flatbedFlatbed TrailerAn open platform trailer (48' or 53' long) without sides or roof, used for freight requiring top/side loading or exceeding enclosed trailer dimensions. shipments.

"I know we handle your dry van freight. We also have excellent flatbed carrier relationships. May I quote those loads as well?"

Different Service Levels

Customer uses truckload but some shipments might work better as LTLLess-Than-Truckload (LTL)Freight service consolidating shipments from multiple shippers (typically 150-10,000 pounds), priced by freight class based on density, stowability, handling, and liability..

"Looking at your smaller shipments, you might save money using LTL consolidation. On shipments under 5,000 pounds, LTL could reduce costs 20-30%. Would you like me to analyze which shipments might benefit?"

Different Divisions or Locations

Customer has multiple facilities. You currently serve one.

"You mentioned your Phoenix facility. Are their shipping needs similar? I'd be happy to support them as well if you'd be comfortable making an introduction."

Keys to Successful Upselling and Cross-Selling

  • Only suggest genuinely beneficial services: Don't recommend what they don't need just to increase revenue
  • Time suggestions appropriately: Wait until you've proven value before suggesting additions
  • Frame as opportunities, not pressure: "Would this be valuable?" beats "You need to do this"
  • Connect to stated needs: When customers mention challenges, connect your additional services to solutions
  • Make it easy to try: Suggest pilot programs rather than major commitments
💡 Pro Tip
The best upselling and cross-selling happens naturally when you're genuinely focused on solving customer problems. If you're paying attention to their operations and challenges, opportunities to provide additional value become obvious.

Practical application

Questions that uncover expansion opportunities:

  • "What other lanes do you ship that we're not currently handling?" Identifies additional lane opportunities.
  • "Do you have other facilities or divisions with shipping needs?" Opens doors to new locations.
  • "Are there services you wish you had that we haven't discussed?" Reveals unmet needs.
  • "If your volume increased, what rate improvements would make a difference?" Explores volume commitments.

Where to focus

For new agents, prioritize:

  • Earn the right first: Prove yourself on initial business before suggesting expansion.
  • Only suggest genuine value: Don't recommend services they don't need.
  • Make it easy to try: Suggest pilots rather than major commitments.

The best upselling happens naturally when you're focused on solving customer problems. Pay attention to their operations and opportunities become obvious.
