Hours of Service Exemptions and Special Cases

While standard HOS rules apply to most freight operations, several exemptions exist for specific situations.

Short-Haul Exemption

Drivers operating within 150 air-miles of their starting location and returning to the starting point within 14 hours may be exempt from certain HOS requirements including:

  • Electronic Logging Device (ELDElectronic Logging Device (ELD)Devices that automatically record driving hours, engine status, and vehicle movement, mandated since 2017 to ensure HOS compliance.) requirements
  • 30-minute break requirement
  • Detailed logs (though they must maintain time records)

This exemption primarily affects local drivers, not long-haul operations you'll typically book. However, understanding it helps explain why some local carriersCarrierA company or individual that transports goods from one place to another, including asset-based carriers, owner-operators, and fleet owners. operate differently than over-the-road carriers.

Adverse Driving Conditions Exception

When drivers encounter adverse driving conditions not apparent when the trip began (snow storms, fog, accidents causing traffic), they may extend the 11-hour driving limit by up to 2 hours to reach a safe location.

"Adverse conditions" must be:

  • Unexpected at trip start (checking weather forecasts means anticipated snow isn't "adverse conditions")
  • Creating danger or significantly slowing progress
  • Legitimate reasons to extend driving time

This exception provides some flexibility for genuine emergencies but cannot be planned around or used routinely.

Agricultural Commodity Exemptions

During planting and harvest seasons, drivers transporting agricultural commodities within 150 air-miles of the source may operate under relaxed HOS rules.

This exemption recognizes the time-sensitive nature of agricultural products during harvest when delays can cause spoilage or lost crops.

If you work with agricultural customers, understand these exemptions allow greater flexibility during peak seasons but don't apply to all agricultural freight—only commodities transported from source during planting/harvest periods.

Emergency Exemptions

During declared emergencies (natural disasters, public health crises, civil emergencies), FMCSAFMCSA (Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration)The primary regulatory body for trucking, issuing operating authority, enforcing safety regulations, and maintaining carrier safety ratings. may grant temporary HOS exemptions allowing:

  • Extended driving hours
  • Relaxed rest requirements
  • Expedited operations for emergency relief

These exemptions are temporary and apply only to freight directly supporting emergency relief efforts.

★ Key Takeaway
Exemptions exist for specific situations but shouldn't be assumed or planned around. Most freight moves under standard HOS rules. When exemptions might apply, carriers and drivers determine eligibility—not brokersBrokerageA company holding FMCSA authority to arrange transportation as a property broker, providing legal authority, technology, back-office functions, and credit resources to agents. or agents.

Practical application

Questions to consider when exemptions might apply:

  • "Is this local or over-the-road?" Short-haul exemptions apply within 150 air-miles of the starting point.
  • "Is this agricultural product during harvest season?" Agricultural exemptions may allow more flexibility.
  • "Is there a declared emergency affecting this route?" Emergency exemptions may temporarily relax HOS for relief freight.

Where to focus

For new agents, prioritize:

  • Standard rules first: Most freight moves under standard HOS rules. Exemptions are the exception, not the norm.
  • Don't assume exemptions: Carriers and drivers determine eligibility, not brokers or agents.
  • Ask when relevant: If a customer mentions agricultural or emergency freight, ask if exemptions might apply.

Understanding exemptions helps you recognize when special circumstances might allow more flexibility, but don't plan around exemptions you can't guarantee.
