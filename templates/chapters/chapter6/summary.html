Chapter 6

Summary Sheet

Hours of Service (HOSHours of Service (HOS)Federal regulations limiting driver operating hours: 11-hour driving limit, 14-hour duty window, 60/70-hour work week, 30-minute break requirement.)

  • 11-hour driving limit: After 10 hours off duty
  • 14-hour duty window: Consecutive, starts at beginning of work
  • 60/70-hour work week: Requires 34-hour restart
  • 30-minute break: Required before 8 hours of driving
  • ELD mandate: Electronic logs eliminate flexibility, strict enforcement
  • Transit time: ~500-550 miles per day realistic; account for loading/unloading consuming 14-hour window

Detention

  • Standard free time: 2 hours pickup and delivery
  • Beyond free time: $25-75/hour detentionDetentionTime drivers spend waiting beyond agreed 'free time' (typically 2 hours) at shipping or receiving facilities, usually charged at $25-75 per hour. charges
  • Impact: Consumes 14-hour window and weekly hours availability
  • Best practice: Communicate expectations clearly when booking

Weight Regulations

  • Federal limits: 80,000 lbs gross, 12K steer axle, 34K drive axles, 34K trailer axles
  • Distribution critical: Not just total weight—each axle set must be within limits
  • State variations: Some allow higher (Michigan up to 160K), some restrict lower
  • Penalties: Escalating fines ($100-$5,000+), possible impoundment, CSA score damage
  • Best practice: Be conservative, confirm weight includes pallets/packaging

Dimensional Restrictions

  • Legal without permits: 8.5' wide, 13.5-14' tall, 53' long, 3-4' rear overhang
  • Slightly oversizeOversize/Overweight (OS/OW)Freight exceeding legal dimensions (8.5' wide, 13.5-14' tall, 53' long) or weight (80,000 lbs), requiring special permits and possibly escorts.: Routine permits, $100-300/state, quick processing
  • Moderately oversize: Route surveys may be needed, $300-800/state, days-weeks
  • Extremely oversize: Engineering review, $1,000s/state, weeks-months, police escorts
  • Critical: Lead time essential—don't accept immediate pickups for permitted loads

Hazardous Materials

  • Nine DOTDOT (Department of Transportation)The federal parent agency overseeing transportation policy, including FMCSA and other modal administrations. classes: Explosives, gases, flammable liquids/solids, oxidizers, toxic/infectious, radioactive, corrosives, miscellaneous
  • Driver requirements: HazmatHazmat (Hazardous Materials)Regulated substances posing risks to health, safety, property, or environment, classified into nine DOT classes and requiring special handling. endorsement (testing, TSA background check), training every 3 years
  • Shipping papers: Proper shipping name, hazard class, UN/NA number, packing group, quantity, emergency contact
  • Placarding: Table 1 (any quantity), Table 2 (over 1,001 lbs aggregate)
  • Insurance: $5M minimum (vs. $750K general freight)
  • Rates: 15-30% premium typical

Food Safety (FSMA)

  • Vehicles: Designed to prevent contamination, properly maintained, adequately cleaned
  • Temperature control: Pre-coolingPre-CoolingBringing a refrigerated trailer to required temperature before loading begins, essential since reefer units maintain temperature but don't rapidly cool warm freight. required, monitoring during transport, recording when needed
  • Contamination prevention: Segregation from non-food, protection during loading/unloading
  • Cleaning: Between loads when necessary to prevent cross-contamination
  • Records: Training, procedures, temperature monitoring, cleaning (12 months minimum)

HOS Exemptions

  • Short-haul: 150 air-miles, return within 14 hours (local operations)
  • Adverse conditions: +2 hours driving when unexpected conditions arise
  • Agricultural: Relaxed rules during planting/harvest within 150 air-miles of source
  • Emergency: Temporary exemptions during declared emergencies

Insurance and Liability

  • Carrier minimum: $750K general freight, $5M hazmat
  • Broker bond: $75K surety bond/trust fund (brokerage maintains)
  • Cargo insurance: Not mandated but common, typically $100K per occurrence
  • Carrier selection liability: BrokersBrokerAn individual or company licensed by the FMCSA to arrange transportation of freight between shippers and carriers without owning trucks. Brokers find capacity, negotiate rates, and coordinate shipments, earning revenue from the margin between what shippers pay and what carriers receive. liable for using unfit/uninsured carriers
  • Carmack Amendment: Governs interstate freight claims (filing requirements, time limits)

Special Delivery Requirements

  • Liftgate: 3,000-5,000 lb capacityCapacityThe availability of trucks relative to loads needing transport; tight capacity (more loads than trucks) increases rates, loose capacity (more trucks than loads) decreases rates., $50-100 charge
  • Residential: $75-150 premium (limited maneuverability, no equipment)
  • Limited access: $50-100 charge (construction sites, farms, schools)
  • Inside delivery: $100-300+ (labor-intensive, not all carriersCarrierA company or individual that transports goods from one place to another, including asset-based carriers, owner-operators, and fleet owners. offer)
  • Appointments: Required at many facilities, fees if missed

Cross-Border (U.S.-Canada/Mexico)

  • Customs documentation: Commercial invoice, USMCA certificate, export declarations
  • Carrier authority: Must have country-specific authority beyond U.S. domestic
  • Driver documentation: Passports, FAST cards for frequent crossers
  • Border delays: Build extra time into estimates (inspections, traffic, random searches)
  • Drayage: Common model for cross-border moves
★ Remember
Regulations exist for safety and infrastructure protection. Understanding them helps you set realistic expectations, avoid violations, and build credibility with customers and carriers. You don't need to be a regulatory expert, but knowing the basics prevents costly mistakes.
