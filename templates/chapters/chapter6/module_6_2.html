Hours of Service (HOS) Regulations

Hours of Service regulations limit how long drivers can operate commercial vehicles before taking mandatory rest breaks. While carriersCarrierA company or individual that transports goods from one place to another, including asset-based carriers, owner-operators, and fleet owners. and drivers are responsible for HOS compliance, understanding these rules helps you set realistic transit time expectations and recognize when customers are requesting impossible schedules.

Key HOS Limits for Property-Carrying Drivers

11-Hour Driving Limit

Drivers may drive a maximum of 11 hours after 10 consecutive hours off duty.

This means once a driver starts their day, they can drive for up to 11 hours before they must stop for a mandatory rest period. Those 11 hours don't need to be consecutive—drivers can take breaks during the 14-hour window—but total driving cannot exceed 11 hours.

14-Hour Duty Limit

After coming on duty, drivers cannot drive beyond the 14th consecutive hour, regardless of how much time was spent not driving.

⚠ Important
The 14-hour clock starts when the driver begins any work-related activity (pretrip inspection, fueling, loading) and runs continuously. Even if a driver only drives 6 hours but spends 8 hours at loading docks, their 14-hour window closes 14 hours after they started their day.

Example: Driver starts duty at 6:00 AM. They can drive until 8:00 PM (14 hours later) regardless of how many of those hours were spent driving versus waiting. If they spent 4 hours in detentionDetentionTime drivers spend waiting beyond agreed 'free time' (typically 2 hours) at shipping or receiving facilities, usually charged at $25-75 per hour. during loading, they only have 10 hours available for driving instead of 11.

60/70-Hour Work Week Limit

Drivers cannot drive after 60 hours on duty in 7 consecutive days, or 70 hours on duty in 8 consecutive days (which limit applies depends on the carrier's operating schedule).

Once a driver reaches this weekly limit, they cannot drive until they take a 34-hour restart period (34 consecutive hours off duty) which resets the weekly clock to zero.

This weekly limit affects driver availability. A driver who's been running hard all week may be unavailable for your load because they've exhausted their weekly hours.

30-Minute Break Requirement

Drivers must take a 30-minute break before 8 hours of driving time. This break can be taken at any point during the first 8 hours of driving.

💡 Pro Tip
A realistic driving day covers approximately 500-550 miles. When calculating transit times, account for the 14-hour duty window being consumed by loading, unloading, and mandatory breaks—not just driving time.

Practical application

Questions to ask when estimating transit times:

  • "How far is this haul?" At 500-550 miles per day, calculate realistic driving days needed.
  • "What are the loading and unloading times?" Every hour at a dock consumes the 14-hour window.
  • "Has the driver been running all week?" Weekly limits may affect availability for long hauls late in the week.
  • "Is this timeline physically possible?" If the math doesn't work with HOS limits, the timeline isn't realistic.

Where to focus

For new agents, prioritize:

  • The 14-hour rule: Understand that this clock runs continuously once it starts, regardless of driving time.
  • Daily mileage limits: 500-550 miles is a realistic driving day. Don't promise more.
  • Detention impact: Every hour waiting reduces driving time available that day.

Understanding HOS prevents you from promising impossible timelines. When a customer asks for 1,200 miles in one day with a solo driver, you'll know that's not legal.
