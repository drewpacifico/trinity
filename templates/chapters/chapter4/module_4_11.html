Module 4.11

Communicating Equipment Needs to Carriers

Once you've determined appropriate equipment, communicating requirements clearly to carriers prevents misunderstandings and problems.

Essential Information to Provide

When contacting carriers, include:

  • Equipment type: "53-foot dry vanDry Van TrailerThe most common trailer type: an enclosed rectangular box (53'×8.5'×9' interior) with 43,000-45,000 lb capacity, used for general freight not requiring temperature control." or "48-foot flatbedFlatbed TrailerAn open platform trailer (48' or 53' long) without sides or roof, used for freight requiring top/side loading or exceeding enclosed trailer dimensions. with tarps" or "53-foot reeferRefrigerated Trailer (Reefer)Insulated trailers with refrigeration units maintaining temperatures from -20°F to +70°F. Essential for produce, dairy, meat, and temperature-sensitive goods. set to 38 degrees"
  • Weight and dimensions: "42,000 pounds, 26 pallets, each pallet 48×40×60 inches tall" or "28,000 pounds, 3 pieces, largest is 24 feet long × 8 feet wide × 6 feet tall"
  • Special requirements: "Food-grade trailer required, no previous chemical loads" or "Straps and edge protectors needed" or "Driver must have hazmatHazmat (Hazardous Materials)Regulated substances posing risks to health, safety, property, or environment, classified into nine DOT classes and requiring special handling. endorsement"
  • Loading/unloading details: "Dock-height pickup and delivery" or "ShipperShipperA business needing to move goods from one location to another, including manufacturers, distributors, retailers, and other customer types. has forklift, receiver needs liftgateLiftgateHydraulic platform attached to trailers that raises and lowers freight at locations lacking loading docks, with 3,000-5,000 pound capacity limits." or "Crane available for loading"
  • Specific instructions: "Pre-cool to 38 degrees before loading" or "Driver must call 2 hours before arrival" or "Freight cannot get wet—secure tarps properly"
💡 Pro Tip
Providing complete information upfront saves time and prevents carriers from arriving unprepared. Never assume carriers will "figure it out"—be specific about every requirement.

Example Equipment Communications

Good communication:

"I need a 53-foot dry van for 42,000 pounds of palletized auto parts. 26 pallets, each 48×40×48 inches. Dock-height pickup in Detroit, dock-height delivery in Nashville. Standard pallet jack for the driver to assist with unloading."

Poor communication:

"Need a truck to move some parts from Detroit to Nashville."

The first example gives carriers everything they need to determine if they can handle the load. The second wastes time with back-and-forth questions.

When Requirements Are Unusual

For loads with unusual requirements, communicate those requirements prominently:

  • "This is hazmat—driver needs hazmat endorsement and we need to verify placarding requirements"
  • "OversizeOversize/Overweight (OS/OW)Freight exceeding legal dimensions (8.5' wide, 13.5-14' tall, 53' long) or weight (80,000 lbs), requiring special permits and possibly escorts. load—14 feet wide, will need permits and possibly escort vehicles"
  • "High-value electronics—require team driversTeam DriversTwo qualified drivers alternating driving and resting, allowing near-continuous movement covering 1,000-1,200+ miles in 24 hours at 50-75% premium rates. and GPS tracking, no unattended parking"
  • "Liquid food-grade—tankerTanker TrailerTrailers hauling liquids (fuel, chemicals, food products) with 5,000-9,000 gallon capacity, heavily regulated especially for hazmat and food-grade. must be kosher certified"

Unusual requirements affect which carriers can handle the load and what rates they'll charge. Be upfront about these requirements rather than surprising carriers after they've committed.

Practical application

Carrier communication checklist:

  • Equipment type and specs: "53-foot dry van" or "48-foot flatbed with tarps"
  • Weight and dimensions: Total weight, piece count, largest piece dimensions
  • Loading details: Dock height, forklift available, liftgate needed
  • Special requirements: Temperature, hazmat, food-grade, securement
  • Any unusual factors: Permits needed, driver certifications, security requirements

Where to focus

For new agents, prioritize:

  • Be specific, not vague: "42,000 pounds of palletized auto parts" beats "some freight."
  • Include loading method: Carriers need to know dock vs. ground level, forklift vs. liftgate.
  • Disclose unusual requirements early: Surprises at pickup frustrate carriers and damage relationships.

Clear communication builds carrier trust. Carriers who know exactly what to expect become reliable partners who prioritize your loads.
