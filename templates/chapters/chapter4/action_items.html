Chapter 4

Action Items

Complete these tasks to master equipment knowledge:

  • Memorize standard dry vanDry Van TrailerThe most common trailer type: an enclosed rectangular box (53'×8.5'×9' interior) with 43,000-45,000 lb capacity, used for general freight not requiring temperature control. dimensions: Quiz yourself until you can recall instantly: 53' × 8.5' × 9', 43-45K lbs, 4,000 cu ft
  • Learn to calculate cubing out vs. weighing out: Practice with example loads: 24 pallets of furniture @ 400 lbs each vs. 22 pallets of batteries @ 2,000 lbs each
  • Study temperature requirements: Create a list of common products and their temperatures (frozen foods, dairy, produce types)
  • Research flatbedFlatbed TrailerAn open platform trailer (48' or 53' long) without sides or roof, used for freight requiring top/side loading or exceeding enclosed trailer dimensions. securing requirements: Understand federal regulations on load securing, when chains vs. straps are appropriate
  • Learn flatbed variants: Be able to explain when to use standard flatbed vs. step deckStep Deck (Drop Deck) TrailerA flatbed with two deck levels (higher front section, lower rear section at ~3.5 feet off ground), accommodating 10-11 foot tall freight. vs. double dropDouble Drop (Lowboy) TrailerA flatbed with three deck levels including a very low center section (18-24 inches off ground), allowing 12-15+ foot tall freight. vs. Conestoga
  • Research equipment rates in your market: Check load boardsLoad BoardOnline marketplace connecting available loads with available trucks (DAT, Truckstop.com, 123Loadboard), also providing market rate intelligence. or talk to carriersCarrierA company or individual that transports goods from one place to another, including asset-based carriers, owner-operators, and fleet owners. about rate differences between dry van, reeferRefrigerated Trailer (Reefer)Insulated trailers with refrigeration units maintaining temperatures from -20°F to +70°F. Essential for produce, dairy, meat, and temperature-sensitive goods., and flatbed
  • Create a quick reference chart: Build a one-page reference showing equipment types, capacities, and ideal freight types
  • Practice equipment matching: Take sample loads from load boards and determine appropriate equipment for each
  • Memorize weight limits: 80K gross, 12K steer, 34K drive, 34K trailer—these numbers should become automatic
  • Learn to communicate equipment needs: Practice writing complete equipment descriptions with all specifications
  • Identify equipment specialists: Find carriers who specialize in flatbed, reefer, heavy haul in your area and begin building relationships
  • Visit a truck stop: See equipment in person—look at different trailer types, talk to drivers about their equipment
