Truck Classifications and Weight Ratings

Understanding truck classifications helps you communicate knowledgeably with carriersCarrierA company or individual that transports goods from one place to another, including asset-based carriers, owner-operators, and fleet owners. and customers about appropriate equipment.

Gross Vehicle Weight Rating (GVWR)

GVWR is the maximum total weight of the vehicle including the vehicle itself plus all cargo. It's the key metric determining truck classification.

Class 8 Trucks (GVWR over 33,000 pounds)

These are standard over-the-road semi-trucks—the heavy-duty tractors pulling trailers you see on highways. This is what you'll be booking for truckload freight.

Class 8 includes:

  • Day cabs (no sleeping compartment)
  • Sleeper cabs (with beds for long-haul drivers)
  • Various axle configurations (tandem, tri-axle, etc.)

Class 7 Trucks (GVWR 26,001-33,000 pounds)

Medium-duty trucks used primarily for local deliveries. Box trucksBox TruckA straight truck with an enclosed cargo area attached directly to the cab. Common sizes range from 10-26 feet, used for local and regional deliveries., delivery trucks, and smaller commercial vehicles fall into this class. You might occasionally book these for local moves or smaller shipments.

Class 6 and Below (GVWR under 26,001 pounds)

Light-duty commercial trucks including delivery trucks, large pickups, small box trucks, and sprinter vansSprinter VanA cargo van (12-15 feet long, 3,000-5,000 lb capacity) used for expedited hot shot deliveries and small urgent shipments. No CDL required.. These typically handle local deliveries, small shipments, or expedited hot shot freight rather than over-the-road truckload.

Why Classification Matters

★ Key Takeaway
Commercial driver's license (CDL) requirements change based on vehicle class. Drivers operating Class 8 trucks need CDLs. This affects carrier availability and rates—CDL drivers command higher wages than non-CDL drivers.

Additionally, some regulations apply differently to different weight classes. Understanding classifications helps you discuss equipment intelligently and understand why certain equipment costs more.

Practical application

Questions related to truck classifications:

  • "Does this load require a full-size semi?" Some smaller loads might work with Class 6-7 trucks at lower cost.
  • "Does the carrier have CDL drivers?" Class 8 operations require CDL-licensed drivers.
  • "Is this local or over-the-road?" Day cabs work for local; sleeper cabs for long haul.

Where to focus

For new agents, prioritize:

  • Class 8 is your default: Most truckload freight moves on Class 8 semi-trucks. Know this category well.
  • CDL requirement: Understand that Class 8 drivers need CDLs, which affects carrier availability and rates.
  • Day cab vs. sleeper: Learn the difference and when each is appropriate based on trip length.

Truck classification knowledge helps you sound professional in carrier conversations and understand equipment cost differences.
