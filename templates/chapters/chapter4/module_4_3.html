Refrigerated Trailers (Reefers)

Refrigerated trailers (universally called "reefers" in the industry) are insulated trailers equipped with refrigeration units that maintain specific temperatures. Essential for transporting temperature-sensitive products, reefers represent a significant segment of the freight market.

Reefer Specifications

  • External Dimensions: Same as dry vansDry Van TrailerThe most common trailer type: an enclosed rectangular box (53'×8.5'×9' interior) with 43,000-45,000 lb capacity, used for general freight not requiring temperature control. (53' × 8.5' × 13.5' tall)
  • Internal Dimensions: Slightly smaller (typically 51-52 feet long due to insulation and refrigeration unit)
  • Weight CapacityCapacityThe availability of trucks relative to loads needing transport; tight capacity (more loads than trucks) increases rates, loose capacity (more trucks than loads) decreases rates.: 40,000-42,000 pounds (less than dry vans due to heavier trailer weight)
  • Temperature Range: -20°F to +70°F
  • Fuel Consumption: 0.5-1.0 gallon per hour (separate diesel tank for refrigeration unit)

Temperature Requirements by Product Type

Different products require different temperatures. Understanding these requirements helps you communicate knowledgeably:

Frozen Products

  • Ice cream and frozen desserts: -10°F to -20°F
  • Frozen vegetables and fruits: 0°F
  • Frozen meat and seafood: 0°F or below
  • General frozen foods: 0°F to -10°F

Fresh Products

  • Fresh meat and poultry: 28°F to 32°F (as cold as possible without freezing)
  • Dairy products: 34°F to 38°F
  • Lettuce and leafy greens: 32°F to 34°F
  • Tomatoes: 50°F to 55°F (will be damaged if too cold)
  • Bananas: 56°F to 58°F
  • Apples: 30°F to 32°F
  • Berries: 31°F to 32°F
  • Pharmaceuticals: Often 36°F to 46°F (2°C to 8°C)
⚠ Important
Always confirm the exact temperature requirement. "Keep it cold" isn't sufficient instruction. CarriersCarrierA company or individual that transports goods from one place to another, including asset-based carriers, owner-operators, and fleet owners. need specific set-point temperatures in degrees.

Operating Modes

  • Continuous cycle: The unit runs constantly, maintaining very stable temperatures. Higher fuel consumption but maximum temperature stability.
  • Start-stop (cycle sentry): The unit cycles on and off to maintain temperature within a range. Saves fuel but creates more temperature fluctuation.

Pre-Cooling: A Critical Requirement

Refrigeration units maintain temperature; they don't rapidly cool warm freight. The trailer must be pre-cooled to the required temperature before loading begins.

Loading warm freight into a warm trailer creates serious problems. Even with the refrigeration unit running at full capacity, it may take many hours to bring the product temperature down—potentially compromising product quality or safety.

However, when dispatching reefer loads, confirm: "Please ensure the trailer is pre-cooled to [temperature] before arriving for loading."

Rate Implications

Reefer freight typically commands 20-40% higher rates than dry van freight for several reasons:

  • Specialized equipment is less available (fewer reefer trailers means tighter capacity)
  • Fuel costs are significant (refrigeration units consume 0.5-1.0 gallons per hour)
  • Maintenance costs are higher (refrigeration units require regular maintenance)
  • Risk is greater (temperature failures can destroy entire loads)
  • Driver expertise matters (not all drivers understand proper reefer operation)
★ Key Takeaway
When pricing reefer loads, account for these factors. Customers expecting dry van rates for refrigerated service have unrealistic expectations you'll need to manage.

Common Reefer Freight Challenges

  • Temperature disputes: Customer claims freight arrived at wrong temperature. Always document set-point temperatures and pre-cooling confirmation.
  • Equipment failures: Refrigeration units break down. Ensure carriers have contingency plans and proper insurance.
  • Loading delays: Extended loading times in warm warehouses can raise product temperature.
  • Receiver rejection: Some receivers test product temperature on arrival and reject loads if outside specification.

Practical application

Questions to ask when booking reefer loads:

  • "What exact temperature do you need?" Get a specific number in degrees, not "cold" or "frozen."
  • "Is this continuous cycle or start-stop acceptable?" Some products need constant temperature.
  • "What product is this?" Knowing the commodity helps verify the temperature requirement makes sense.
  • "Does the carrier need to provide a temperature recorder printout?" Some customers require proof of temperature maintenance.

Where to focus

For new agents, prioritize:

  • Temperature specificity: Always get exact temperatures. "Keep it cold" causes problems.
  • Pre-cooling confirmation: Remind carriers to pre-cool before pickup. This prevents most temperature disputes.
  • Lower weight capacity: Remember reefers carry 40,000-42,000 pounds, not the 45,000 pounds of dry vans.

Reefer freight commands premium rates but requires more attention to detail. Master the basics before taking on high-value pharmaceutical or specialty food loads.
