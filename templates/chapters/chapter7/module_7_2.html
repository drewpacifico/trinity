Customer Types by Business Model

Different business models create different shipping patterns and needs. Understanding these differences helps you target prospects strategically.

Manufacturers

Manufacturers produce goods requiring regular shipment to distributors, retailers, or other facilities. They're often excellent long-term customers.

Strengths as customers:

  • Predictable shipping schedules aligned with production cycles
  • Consistent volumes creating stable revenue streams
  • Often ship same lanes repeatedly, allowing you to develop specialized expertise
  • Understand logistics complexities from experience
  • Value reliability because production schedules depend on coordinated freight

Challenges you'll face:

  • Many have established carrierCarrierA company or individual that transports goods from one place to another, including asset-based carriers, owner-operators, and fleet owners. relationships built over years
  • Often have sophisticated procurement processes with competitive bidding
  • Price-sensitive because freight represents significant cost
  • May have stringent requirements for carrier qualifications and insurance
  • Decision cycles can be long (weeks or months to become approved vendors)
💡 Pro Tip
Position yourself as capacityCapacityThe availability of trucks relative to loads needing transport; tight capacity (more loads than trucks) increases rates, loose capacity (more trucks than loads) decreases rates. supplement rather than trying to replace primary carriers immediately. Request opportunities to quote overflow or backup freight. Once you've proven yourself on a few shipments, gradually expand your share of their business.

Distributors and Wholesalers

Distributors purchase products from manufacturers and sell to retailers or other businesses. They operate warehouses shipping products to multiple destinations regularly.

Strengths as customers:

  • High shipping volumes creating substantial commission opportunities
  • Multiple destinations providing diverse lane opportunities
  • Less seasonal than manufacturers
  • Value efficiency and speed
  • Often willing to work with multiple freight providers simultaneously

Challenges you'll face:

  • Intense price sensitivity (freight is major expense affecting margins)
  • May use multiple brokersBrokerageA company holding FMCSA authority to arrange transportation as a property broker, providing legal authority, technology, back-office functions, and credit resources to agents. and carriers, creating competition for each load
  • High service expectations—need fast quotes, quick confirmations
  • Payment terms might be longer (net 45 or net 60)

Retailers

Retailers sell products to consumers through physical stores or online platforms. They need regular inbound shipments and sometimes outbound shipments between locations.

Best approach: Focus on small to mid-sized retailers ($5M-$100M revenue) who lack dedicated logistics departments. Understand their receiving constraints and build carrier relationships with companies willing to work within strict parameters.

Construction Companies

Construction companies move equipment, materials, and supplies to job sites. These shipments often involve heavy, oversized, or irregularly shaped items requiring specialized equipment.

Strengths as customers:

  • Need specialized equipment (flatbedsFlatbed TrailerAn open platform trailer (48' or 53' long) without sides or roof, used for freight requiring top/side loading or exceeding enclosed trailer dimensions., step decksStep Deck (Drop Deck) TrailerA flatbed with two deck levels (higher front section, lower rear section at ~3.5 feet off ground), accommodating 10-11 foot tall freight.) commanding higher rates
  • Willing to pay premium for reliable service
  • Value expertise in handling challenging freight
  • Often need urgent shipments when plans change

Challenges: Irregular patterns tied to projects, challenging job site deliveries, seasonal fluctuations, sometimes slow payment.

E-commerce Businesses

Online retailers represent a rapidly growing segment with complex logistics needs.

Strengths: Fast-growing, often lack established relationships, volume can grow quickly.

Challenges: Expectations may be unrealistic, unpredictable volumes, uncertain creditworthiness for newer companies.

⚠ Important
E-commerce customers often expect parcel carrier service levels at truckload prices. Educate them on freight logistics realities while demonstrating flexibility and responsiveness. Start with smaller commitments and grow with them.

Agricultural Businesses

Farms, cooperatives, and agricultural processors move crops, livestock, feed, equipment, and supplies with unique characteristics driven by seasonal cycles.

Best approach: Build relationships before peak season and maintain them year-round. Develop carriers willing to serve rural areas. Be flexible with timing because weather often disrupts schedules.

Practical application

Questions to ask when evaluating different customer types:

  • "What does your production or sales cycle look like?" Understand their shipping patterns and peak periods.
  • "How do you currently arrange your trucking?" Learn about existing relationships and potential gaps.
  • "What challenges have you faced with freight?" Identify problems you can solve.
  • "Would you be open to working with a backup provider?" Position yourself as supplement, not replacement.

Where to focus

For new agents, prioritize:

  • Start with manufacturers: They offer predictable volumes, consistent lanes, and value reliability.
  • Position as backup first: Request overflow opportunities to prove yourself before seeking primary business.

Understanding business models helps you approach each prospect appropriately and identify where you can add the most value.
