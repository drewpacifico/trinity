How Money Flows Through the Industry

Understanding the financial flow helps you manage expectations, explain payment processes to customers and carriers, and recognize where risks exist. Money doesn't move instantly in this industry, and the timing matters.

The basic transaction flow

Here's how money moves through a typical freight transaction:

  1. You quote the customer: Based on the shipment details, you provide a rate to your customer (the shipper).
  2. You negotiate with a carrier: You find a carrier willing to haul the load for less than your customer rate.
  3. The load delivers: The carrier completes the shipment and submits paperwork.
  4. Your brokerage invoices the customer: The customer receives an invoice for the agreed rate.
  5. Your brokerage pays the carrier: According to your payment terms with the carrier.
  6. The customer pays your brokerage: According to their payment terms.
  7. You receive your commission: Your share of the margin, per your agreement with the brokerage.

Payment timing

The timing of payments creates a cash flow challenge that's important to understand:

Customer payment terms

  • Net 15 to Net 60: Customers typically pay 15 to 60 days after delivery.
  • Net 30 is common: Many customers negotiate net 30 as their standard terms.
  • Larger customers often pay slower: Big companies may insist on net 45 or net 60.

Carrier payment expectations

  • Net 7 to Net 30: Carriers expect payment faster than customers pay.
  • Quick pay options: Some carriers accept a small discount (2-3%) for payment within 5-7 days.
  • Factoring: Many carriers sell their invoices to factoring companies for immediate cash, meaning the factoring company collects from your brokerage.
★ The Timing Mismatch
Your brokerage pays carriers before customers pay you. If a customer pays net 30 but the carrier expects net 7, the brokerage covers that 23-day gap. This is why brokerages need working capital and why customer creditworthiness matters.

Where your commission comes from

Your commission comes from the gross margin on each load. Here's how it breaks down:

  • Customer pays: $1,500
  • Carrier receives: $1,100
  • Gross margin: $400

From this $400 margin, the brokerage covers:

  • Overhead costs: Office, technology, insurance, administrative staff
  • Non-payment risk: Reserve for customers who pay late or don't pay
  • Cost of capital: The expense of paying carriers before collecting from customers
  • Your commission: Your share based on your split agreement
💰 Commission Calculation Example

At a 50/50 commission split (50% to you, 50% to brokerage):

  • Gross margin: $400
  • Your commission (50%): $200
  • Brokerage share (50%): $200

Your split percentage may vary. Understand your specific agreement.

⚠ Don't Squeeze Carriers Too Hard
Pushing a carrier rate from $1,100 to $1,025 gains you about $37 in commission (at 50% split). But if that carrier provides poor service because they're underpaid, you could lose a customer worth thousands in future business. Margin matters, but so does service quality.

Why this matters to you

  • Customer credit matters: A customer who doesn't pay creates a real loss. Your brokerage has already paid the carrier.
  • Carrier relationships need fair pay: Carriers who feel squeezed won't prioritize your freight or may decline future loads.
  • Cash flow affects everyone: When customers pay slowly, it strains the entire system. Understanding this helps you explain payment policies.

Practical application

Use this knowledge in your daily work:

  • When quoting customers: Factor in their likely payment terms. A net 60 customer costs your brokerage more than a net 15 customer.
  • When negotiating with carriers: Understand that quick pay options exist and may help you secure capacity when rates are close.
  • When a carrier asks about payment: Know your brokerage's standard terms so you can answer confidently.
  • When calculating your earnings: Remember that commission comes from margin, not from the total customer rate.

Where to focus

For new agents, prioritize understanding:

  • The margin calculation: Customer rate minus carrier rate equals gross margin. Your commission is a percentage of that margin.
  • The timing mismatch: Carriers get paid before customers pay. This is normal but creates cash flow needs.
  • Your brokerage's terms: Know what payment terms your brokerage offers to carriers and requires from customers.

Don't worry yet about:

  • Factoring details (your brokerage handles this)
  • Complex payment negotiations (these come with experience)
  • Customer credit analysis (your brokerage's credit team handles this)

Focus on understanding the basic flow. The financial details become intuitive as you book more loads and see the process in action.
