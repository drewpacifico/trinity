Agricultural Businesses

Farms, cooperatives, and agricultural processors move crops, livestock, feed, equipment, and supplies. The defining characteristic of this market is seasonality. Understanding harvest cycles is essential to working with agricultural customers.

Types of agricultural customers

Agricultural businesses vary widely. Knowing the differences helps you ask the right questions:

  • Row crop farms: Corn, soybeans, wheat, cotton. Ship bulk grain to elevators or processors. Extremely seasonal, with intense activity during harvest and quiet periods otherwise.
  • Produce farms: Fruits and vegetables. Require refrigerated trailersRefrigerated Trailer (Reefer)Insulated trailers with refrigeration units maintaining temperatures from -20°F to +70°F. Essential for produce, dairy, meat, and temperature-sensitive goods. and time-sensitive delivery. Spoilage risk means delays are costly.
  • Livestock operations: Cattle, hogs, poultry. Specialized livestock trailers with animal welfare regulations. Most new agents don't work this niche.
  • Dairy farms: Daily milk pickup to processing plants. Tanker equipment, food-grade requirements, strict schedules.
  • Cooperatives and processors: Aggregate from multiple farms. Higher volumes, more consistent shipping, often year-round operations.
🌱 Where New Agents Start

Most new agents working agricultural freight focus on produce (reefer loads) or cooperatives/processors (more consistent volumes). Bulk grain requires hopper trailers and livestock requires specialized equipment. Both are niche markets with dedicated brokers. You'll learn more about equipment types in Chapter 4.

The seasonality factor

Agriculture follows nature's calendar. Understanding when your customers are busiest helps you prospect at the right time and plan for capacity challenges:

  • Spring (March-May): Planting season. Inbound shipments of seed, fertilizer, and equipment. Farmers are busy in fields and hard to reach by phone.
  • Summer (June-August): Produce harvest begins. Fruits and vegetables moving from farms to markets. Reefer capacity tightens. Rates rise.
  • Fall (September-November): Peak harvest for row crops. Grain moving to elevators. Produce season continues. This is the busiest period, and rates can spike 40-60% above off-season.
  • Winter (December-February): Slowest period. Equipment maintenance, planning for next year. Best time to build relationships for the coming season.
💡 Timing Your Outreach

Don't cold-call a farmer during harvest. They're working 16-hour days and won't take your call. The best time to build agricultural relationships is late winter (January-February) when farmers are planning their year and have time to talk. By the time harvest arrives, you should already be their trusted broker.

Why they're worth the complexity

Despite the challenges, agricultural customers offer real advantages:

  • High-volume seasons: When harvest hits, volumes surge. A single customer can generate significant revenue in a few months.
  • Less competition: Rural locations and specialized requirements discourage many brokers. Agents who understand agriculture face fewer competitors.
  • Loyalty: Farmers value relationships. Once you prove reliable during a critical harvest, they'll call you first next year.
  • Premium rates: Time-sensitive produce and capacity-constrained harvest seasons command higher rates than typical freight.

Challenges you'll face

  • Feast or famine: Extreme seasonality means intense activity during harvest, minimal activity otherwise. Your income will fluctuate.
  • Weather dependence: Rain delays harvest. Early frost damages crops. Drought reduces yields. Plans change constantly.
  • Rural access: Farm locations may have unpaved roads, limited turning space, and no loading docks. Not all carriers will go there.
  • Price sensitivity: Commodity prices fluctuate. When crop prices drop, customers squeeze freight budgets.

Practical application

Questions to ask agricultural customers:

  • "What crops do you produce, and when is your harvest?" This tells you their peak season and equipment needs.
  • "Where does your product go after it leaves your farm?" This identifies lanes and receiver types (elevator, processor, distribution center).
  • "What equipment do you have on-site for loading?" Rural locations may lack forklifts or docks.
  • "How do you currently handle your freight during harvest?" This reveals pain points with current providers.

Regulations you'll encounter

Agricultural freight has specific regulatory considerations you'll learn in detail in Chapter 6:

  • FSMA (Food Safety Modernization Act): Food products require sanitary trailers, temperature documentation, and proper handling procedures.
  • Agricultural HOS exemption: During planting and harvest, drivers transporting agricultural commodities within 150 miles of the source operate under relaxed hours-of-service rules.

Where to focus

If agriculture interests you, consider specializing:

  • Produce logistics: Fruits and vegetables from farm to market. Reefer expertise, time-sensitive delivery, food safety knowledge.
  • Regional grain: If you're in agricultural areas (Midwest, Great Plains), grain movement to local elevators and processors.
  • Agricultural inputs: Seed, fertilizer, and equipment moving TO farms. Less seasonal than outbound crops.

Specialization in agriculture takes time because you need to learn crop cycles, regional patterns, and build carrier relationships before your first harvest season. Plan ahead.
