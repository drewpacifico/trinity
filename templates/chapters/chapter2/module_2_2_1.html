Module 2.2.1

Manufacturers

Manufacturers produce goods that need regular shipment to distributors, retailers, or other facilities. A furniture manufacturer ships finished products to retail stores. An automotive parts producer sends components to assembly plants. A food processor moves packaged goods to distribution centers. For freight agents, manufacturers often make ideal long-term customers because of their consistent shipping patterns.

How manufacturing freight works

Understanding the manufacturing business helps you serve them better:

  • Two types of freight: Manufacturers ship finished goods outbound and receive raw materials and components inbound. Both directions create opportunities.
  • Production schedules drive freight: They produce on regular cycles. If a plant runs three shifts and produces 500 units daily, they ship a predictable number of truckloads weekly.
  • Freight is a cost center: Transportation is a significant expense they track and manage carefully. They negotiate hard because savings go straight to the bottom line.
  • Reliability matters most: A late delivery can shut down a production line. A late shipment can mean missed customer commitments. They pay for reliability.

Why manufacturers make good customers

  • Predictable patterns: Production schedules create consistent freight volumes. You can forecast and plan capacity.
  • Long-term relationships: Once you prove reliable, manufacturers stick with you. They don't want to constantly re-qualify new vendors.
  • They understand logistics: You're not educating them on how freight works. They know what they need and communicate clearly.
  • Growth potential: A small manufacturer shipping 5 loads monthly today might ship 20 monthly in two years. Growing with them compounds your business.
  • Month-end and quarter-end surges: Manufacturers push to meet production targets at period end, creating predictable volume spikes.
⚠ Just-In-Time Delivery

Some manufacturers use just-in-time (JIT) inventory systems. Parts must arrive within precise time windows. Early delivery is often as problematic as late delivery because they lack storage space. Late delivery can shut down a production line costing thousands per hour. If a customer mentions JIT requirements, understand exactly what that means before committing.

Challenges you'll face

  • Established relationships: Most manufacturers have carrier relationships built over years. You're not their first call.
  • Procurement processes: Larger manufacturers have formal vendor qualification, competitive bidding, and contract requirements.
  • Price pressure: Freight is a major cost. They compare rates constantly and expect competitive pricing.
  • Strict requirements: Insurance minimums, safety ratings, equipment specifications. They qualify carriers carefully.
  • High stakes: Miss a delivery and you might shut down a production line. The consequences of failure are significant.
💡 The Capacity Supplement Strategy

Don't try to replace a manufacturer's primary carriers. Instead, position yourself as their capacity supplement. During peak seasons, month-end pushes, or when regular carriers are full, they need backup options. Start as their "Plan B" and prove your value. Over time, you earn more of their business without a head-to-head price fight.

What manufacturers expect from you

  • Reliability above all: Cover the loads you commit to. Every time. No excuses.
  • On-time performance: Production schedules don't flex. Deliveries must arrive within committed windows.
  • Communication: If there's a problem, tell them immediately. Surprises are unacceptable.
  • Qualified carriers: They'll ask about carrier safety ratings, insurance, and equipment. Be prepared to provide documentation.

Practical application

Questions to ask manufacturing prospects:

  • "What do you manufacture, and where does your finished product go?" Reveals freight types and lanes.
  • "How many outbound shipments do you move per week?" Quantifies opportunity size.
  • "Do you have strict delivery windows or just-in-time requirements?" Identifies service level expectations.
  • "How do you handle overflow when your regular carriers are at capacity?" Opens the door to the capacity supplement strategy.
  • "What's your carrier qualification process?" Helps you understand what you need to provide.

Where to focus

For new agents, prioritize:

  • Small to mid-sized manufacturers: Large enough for consistent volume but without corporate procurement bureaucracy. The shipping manager often makes decisions directly.
  • Growing manufacturers: Companies adding production capacity or new product lines. They need additional freight capacity.
  • Specialty manufacturers: Niche products where expertise matters. Less price competition than commodity goods.

Approach with caution:

  • Large manufacturers with corporate procurement and contract requirements
  • JIT operations unless you have reliable carriers who understand tight windows
  • Industries requiring equipment or expertise you don't have

Manufacturers reward consistency and reliability over time. Start as their backup, deliver flawlessly, and earn a larger share of their freight as you prove your value.
