Module 3.4

Operational Execution

After booking a load, your work isn't finished—it's just beginning. Operational execution means ensuring the shipment proceeds smoothly from pickup through delivery.

Tracking and Monitoring

From the moment a load is dispatched until delivery is confirmed, you should know where that truck is and whether it's progressing as planned.

Key tracking milestones:

  • Confirm the carrier picked up on time and the freight was loaded correctly
  • Check in during transit, especially on longer hauls, to verify progress
  • Confirm the delivery appointment is still valid and the receiver is prepared
  • Verify successful delivery and obtain proof of delivery (POD) documentation
💡 Pro Tip
Don't wait for problems to surface. Proactive communication—checking in before issues arise—allows you to address small problems before they become crises. If a driver is running behind schedule, you want to know early so you can notify the customer.

Modern technology helps with tracking, but don't rely solely on automated systems. A five-minute phone call with a driver often reveals important information that GPS tracking can't provide.

Problem Resolution

Despite your best efforts, problems will occur. Trucks break down. Weather delays shipments. Facilities miss appointments. How you handle these situations determines whether customers remain loyal.

When problems arise, follow this process:

  1. Gather complete information immediately. What exactly happened? What's the current status? What are the options for resolution?
  2. Communicate transparently with all parties. Tell your customer about the problem right away, explain what you're doing to resolve it, and provide realistic timelines.
  3. Develop solutions quickly. Can another carrier complete the delivery? Can the appointment be rescheduled? Do you need to arrange transloading?
  4. Follow through until resolution is complete. Stay involved until the freight is delivered and all parties are satisfied.
  5. Follow up after resolution. Call the customer to confirm satisfaction. Learn from what happened to prevent similar problems.
★ Key Takeaway
Customers remember how you handle problems more than they remember perfect shipments. Excellent service recovery often strengthens relationships more than if the problem never occurred.

Documentation Management

Freight transportation creates substantial documentation. Managing it properly protects you legally, facilitates payment, and provides records for resolving disputes.

Essential documents for every shipment:

  • Bills of Lading (BOL): The contract between shipper and carrier specifying what's being shipped and the terms
  • Proof of Delivery (POD): Signed documentation confirming freight was delivered—you need this to invoice customers
  • Rate confirmations: The agreement between you and the carrier specifying rate and terms
  • Inspection reports: For freight requiring quality checks or special handling
  • Freight claims documentation: Photos, inspection reports, BOLs, and repair estimates if damage occurs
⚠ Important
Organize documentation systematically. Three months after delivery, when a customer questions a charge or a carrier disputes payment, you need immediate access to supporting documentation.

Customer Communication

Throughout the shipment lifecycle, proactive communication builds trust and prevents anxious phone calls.

Communication best practices:

  • Send pickup confirmation immediately after the carrier loads
  • Provide mid-transit updates on longer hauls
  • Alert customers immediately to any delays or problems—don't wait for them to call you
  • Send delivery confirmation as soon as you receive it
💡 Pro Tip
Many customers appreciate regular updates even when everything is going perfectly. It reassures them you're monitoring their freight attentively.
