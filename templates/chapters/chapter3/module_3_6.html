Module 3.6

Administrative and Compliance Tasks

Behind-the-scenes administrative work isn't glamorous, but it's essential for operating legally and professionally.

Carrier Vetting

Federal regulations require brokers to verify that carriers have proper authority, insurance, and acceptable safety ratings before tendering freight. This isn't optional—it's a legal requirement that protects you from liability.

Every carrier you use must have:

  • Active FMCSA operating authority (MC number or DOT number)
  • Valid commercial auto liability insurance at required levels ($750,000 minimum for most freight, $5,000,000 for hazardous materials)
  • Acceptable safety rating (avoid carriers with "Unsatisfactory" ratings or consistently poor inspection results)
⚠ Important
Most brokerages have systems for carrier vetting that check these requirements automatically. However, you're responsible for ensuring you only use approved carriers. Using a carrier without proper authority or insurance exposes you to enormous liability if accidents or cargo loss occurs.

Beyond regulatory requirements, evaluate carriers based on their reputation, equipment quality, and reliability. A carrier with legal authority but terrible service damages your customer relationships.

Record Keeping

Maintain organized records of all transactions, communications, and documents. This serves multiple purposes:

  • Financial management: You need records for invoicing, payment processing, and tax preparation
  • Dispute resolution: When disagreements arise months after delivery, detailed records prove what actually happened
  • Regulatory compliance: FMCSA regulations require brokers maintain specific records for defined periods (typically three years)
  • Business analysis: Historical records show trends in customer behavior, carrier performance, and margin evolution
★ Key Takeaway
Whatever system you use—whether sophisticated transportation management software or basic spreadsheets—ensure consistency. Every load should have a standardized file containing all relevant documents and communications.

Regulatory Compliance

Multiple regulations govern freight brokerage operations. While your brokerage's compliance team handles most regulatory requirements, you must understand basic obligations:

  • Broker authority requirements: Brokerages must maintain active FMCSA broker authority and the required $75,000 surety bond or trust fund
  • Cargo claims procedures: The Carmack Amendment establishes procedures and timelines for filing and resolving cargo loss or damage claims
  • Hours of service rules: Understanding driver HOS regulations helps you set realistic transit expectations
  • Hazardous materials regulations: Hazmat shipments require specific documentation, placarding, and carrier qualifications
  • Food safety regulations: FSMA establishes requirements for temperature control, sanitation, and documentation for food shipments

You don't need to become a regulatory expert, but understanding basic requirements helps you avoid violations and protect your customers and carriers.

Technology Management

Modern freight operations depend on technology—transportation management systems, load boards, tracking platforms, communication tools, and document management systems.

Learn to use these tools effectively:

  • Your TMS for quoting, booking, tracking, and invoicing
  • Load boards for finding carriers and researching market rates
  • Email and phone systems for professional communication
  • Document scanning and electronic signature tools for paperwork management
  • Reporting tools for analyzing your business performance

Technology should make your work easier and more efficient, not more complicated. If you're struggling with a system, ask for training.

💡 Pro Tip
Stay informed about new technologies that might improve your operations. The freight industry continues evolving technologically, and agents who adopt useful innovations gain competitive advantages.
