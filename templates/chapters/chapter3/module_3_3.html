Load Booking and Coordination

Once you've established customer relationships, the core of your daily work becomes booking and coordinating shipments. This process has five key steps that must be executed carefully every time.

Gathering Load Details

Every shipment requires specific information before you can price it accurately and find an appropriate carrierCarrierA company or individual that transports goods from one place to another, including asset-based carriers, owner-operators, and fleet owners.. Missing or incorrect details cause problems later, so develop a systematic approach to information gathering.

Essential information includes:

  • Pickup and delivery locations with complete addresses, not just city names. Some cities have multiple facilities for the same company. You need the exact location.
  • Pickup and delivery dates and times. Is this a specific appointment or a window? What are the operating hours? Is there flexibility?
  • Commodity description. What exactly is being shipped? General terms like "freight" or "parts" aren't sufficient. You need to know if it's steel coils, furniture, food products, or machinery.
  • Weight and dimensions. Total weight, number of pieces, and dimensions of the largest piece. For palletized freight, how many pallets and weight per pallet?
  • Special requirements. Temperature control? Hazardous material? Appointment required? LiftgateLiftgateHydraulic platform attached to trailers that raises and lowers freight at locations lacking loading docks, with 3,000-5,000 pound capacity limits. needed? Straps, tarps, or special securing equipment?
  • Contact information for pickup and delivery. Who should the driver call? What are their phone numbers?
⚠ Important
The more complete your information, the more accurate your quote and the fewer problems you'll encounter. Missing details are the number one cause of load booking failures.

Determining Equipment Requirements

Based on the load details, you must identify what type of truck and trailer are needed. This seems straightforward but requires careful consideration.

Ask yourself:

  • Does the freight physically fit in the equipment type I'm considering?
  • Will the weight exceed the equipment's capacityCapacityThe availability of trucks relative to loads needing transport; tight capacity (more loads than trucks) increases rates, loose capacity (more trucks than loads) decreases rates.? (Remember reefersRefrigerated Trailer (Reefer)Insulated trailers with refrigeration units maintaining temperatures from -20°F to +70°F. Essential for produce, dairy, meat, and temperature-sensitive goods. carry less than dry vansDry Van TrailerThe most common trailer type: an enclosed rectangular box (53'×8.5'×9' interior) with 43,000-45,000 lb capacity, used for general freight not requiring temperature control.)
  • Does the freight require temperature control, and if so, what specific temperature?
  • Can the freight be loaded and unloaded at the specified locations with the selected equipment?
⚠ Common Mistake
Getting equipment selection wrong causes serious problems. Dispatching a dry van for freight that needs a flatbedFlatbed TrailerAn open platform trailer (48' or 53' long) without sides or roof, used for freight requiring top/side loading or exceeding enclosed trailer dimensions. means the carrier arrives and cannot load. You've wasted everyone's time and damaged relationships with both customer and carrier.

Pricing the Load

Determining what to charge your customer is part science and part art. You need to cover the carrier's costs plus your margin, while remaining competitive.

Factors affecting your price:

  • Current market rates for the lane. Load boardsLoad BoardOnline marketplace connecting available loads with available trucks (DAT, Truckstop.com, 123Loadboard), also providing market rate intelligence. provide benchmarks, but experience on the lane tells you more than any single data point.
  • Difficulty of the shipment. Easy loads (dock-to-dock, flexible timing) price lower than difficult loads (residential, tight appointments, rural areas).
  • Urgency. Customers needing immediate pickup pay more than those with flexible timing.
  • Relationship and volume. Long-term customers providing consistent volume deserve better pricing than occasional one-off shipments.
  • Your desired margin. You need to earn enough to make the effort worthwhile.
  • Seasonal and market conditions. During tight capacity, rates increase. During loose capacity, rates decrease.
⚠ Common Mistake
New agents often underprice loads because they're afraid of losing business. This creates a cycle of working hard for minimal income. Price confidently based on real market conditions and the value you provide.

Finding a Carrier

Once you've priced the load for your customer, you need to find a carrier willing to haul it at a rate that preserves your margin.

Start with carriers you know and trust who operate in this lane. Carriers who've successfully handled similar freight for you before are your best option because you know their reliability.

If your regular carriers are unavailable, expand your search through load boards, carrier databases, or referrals from other agents.

💡 Pro Tip
When contacting carriers, provide complete information immediately. Instead of "Got a load going to Texas, you interested?" say: "I have 42,000 lbs of palletized freight, 24 pallets, picking up Thursday in Kansas City going to Houston, delivering Friday. ShipperShipperA business needing to move goods from one location to another, including manufacturers, distributors, retailers, and other customer types. and receiver are both warehouses with docks. Rate is $2,100. Are you available and interested?"

Verify every carrier's authority, insurance, and safety rating before tendering freight. Your brokerage should have processes for this, but you're responsible for using only properly vetted carriers.

Confirming the Shipment

Once you've agreed on terms with a carrier, confirm everything in writing. Verbal agreements aren't sufficient—you need documentation protecting all parties.

Rate confirmations typically include:

  • Your contact information and the carrier's contact information
  • Load details (commodity, weight, dimensions, piece count)
  • Pickup information (date, time, location, contact, special instructions)
  • Delivery information (date, time, location, contact, special instructions)
  • Rate and payment terms
  • Any special requirements or accessorialAccessorial ChargesAdditional fees charged by carriers for services beyond standard dock-to-dock delivery, such as liftgate service, inside delivery, residential delivery, or appointment scheduling. charges
  • Insurance requirements

Send rate confirmationsRate Confirmation (Rate Con)Written agreement between broker and carrier specifying load details, rate, payment terms, and special requirements. immediately after booking the load. Delays create opportunities for miscommunication.

⚠ Important
Always require carrier acknowledgment of the rate confirmation. They should sign and return it or confirm receipt electronically. This acknowledgment creates a binding agreement protecting both parties.

Practical application

Questions to ask when gathering load details:

  • "What exactly are you shipping?" Get the commodity description, not just "product" or "freight."
  • "What's the total weight and how is it packaged?" Palletized? Floor-loaded? Piece count?
  • "Are there any access restrictions at pickup or delivery?" Dock height, liftgate needed, forklift available?
  • "What's your flexibility on pickup and delivery timing?" Tight windows cost more than flexible ones.
  • "Is there anything unusual about this shipment I should know?" This catches things customers forget to mention.

Where to focus

For new agents, prioritize:

  • Information gathering discipline: Create a checklist and use it every time. Missing one detail can derail an entire shipment.
  • Equipment matching basics: Learn the fundamental differences between dry vans, flatbeds, and reefers. Know when each is required. You'll learn more about equipment in Chapter 4.
  • Rate confirmation accuracy: Every detail in your rate confirmation must match what you told the customer and carrier. Double-check before sending.

Pricing strategy and carrier negotiation skills develop with experience. Focus first on gathering complete information and executing bookings accurately.
